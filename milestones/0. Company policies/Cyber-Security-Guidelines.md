## What are common cyber security threats in a remote work environment?

1) Phishing - where attacks impersonate entities/people you know through email, text or phone calls to steal senstive information like passwords, financial details, or even install malware. They usually trcik victims into clicking/dowloading suspicious links or attachments.
2) Ransomware - malicious software that encrypts a user's data, and holds them ransom (hence the name) to demand payment.
3) Weak passwords - this can mean failure to use complex and unique passwords and/or multi-factor authentication to safguard senstive data.
4) Shadow IT - workers using unauthorised apps or software on work tasks; which can expose senstive company data. 

## What are best practices for keeping your devices and accounts secure?

1) Complex/unique passwrds and managers - using unique passwords for different personal logins can help make compromises harder. Even better if you have a password manager to help manage different passwords.
2) Multi-factor authentications: A security process where two or more independent proofs of ID is needed - e.g passwords, token numbers, security questions etc.
3) Automatic software updates: Consistent software updates help patch any existing vulnerabilites and prevents pontential threats.
4) Keeping passwords to yourself: no sharing of passwords that may risk exposing personal data into pulic knowledge.

## How should you handle phishing attempts and suspicious links?

1) Do not engage with any potential phishing (emails, calls, links or attachments); even if they appear as entities you know (e.g managers, co-workers, family and friends)
2) Take note of the threat and report (e.g screenshots, filing a report) and then block. This can be done through many different ways depending on the phishing format - like blocking an email.

If a user happens to have engaged with a phishing attempt:
1) Change passwords immediately on any affected accounts.
2) Disconnect your device from the internet to prevent potential malware spread.
3) Contact relevant stakeholders if particular sensitive information has been shared - e.g banks, employers,etc.
4) Scan your computer for any virus with anti-virus softwares.

## What makes a strong password, and why should you use a password manager?

a strong password consists of a mix of upper and lower case letters, numbers and symbols. Ideally it would be 16+ characters long. 
On top of this, the use of a password manager helps store, create and secure unique passwords for every online account you have in a single encrypted location. 

## Why is two-factor authentication (2FA) important, and when should you enable it?

2FAs create a second security blanket to help make your accounts even less liely to be compromised. It stops unauthorised access, phising, and credential stuffy by providing a second level of authentication. This is esecially important for accounts/information that are confidential - like banking apps, social media, cloud storage and email accounts.

## How can you make secure behaviour a habit rather than an afterthought?

1) Create personal reminders and routines. For example, when using my email on a device that isn’t my own, such as at a library, I would set a reminder on my phone to make sure I log out of my account before leaving. Building small routines like this helps reinforce secure behaviour over time.
2) Create checklists for common tasks. For tasks that involve sensitive information or system access, having a simple checklist can ensure that security steps are always followed. For example, confirming that you logged out of systems, locked your device, or saved files in the correct secure location.
3) Stay aware of security risks. Regularly reviewing security guidelines or updates helps keep good security practices top of mind. The more familiar someone is with common risks like phishing or weak passwords, the more naturally they incorporate secure behaviour into their daily work.

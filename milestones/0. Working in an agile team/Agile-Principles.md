## Scrum vs Kanban

### Scrum

Scrum is an Agile framework that organises work into fixed-length iterations called **sprints** (usually 1–4 weeks). At the start of each sprint, the team plans the work they will complete. Scrum has clearly defined roles, including:

- **Product Owner** – prioritises the backlog
- **Scrum Master** – facilitates the process
- **Development Team** – delivers the work

Work is committed at the beginning of the sprint and typically does not change until the sprint ends. Scrum emphasises structured ceremonies such as sprint planning, daily stand-ups, sprint reviews, and retrospectives.

Scrum works best for teams building new features or products where work can be planned in short cycles.

---

### Kanban

Kanban is a continuous workflow method focused on visualising work and limiting **Work In Progress (WIP)**. Unlike Scrum, Kanban does not use fixed-length sprints. Tasks move continuously across a board (e.g., To Do → In Progress → Done).

Key characteristics of Kanban:

- No fixed iterations
- No required roles
- Continuous delivery
- Focus on flow efficiency
- Limits on how many tasks can be in progress at once

Kanban is especially useful for teams handling ongoing work such as bug fixes, backend maintenance, DevOps tasks, or support requests.

---

### Main Differences

- **Structure:** Scrum is structured around sprints and defined roles, while Kanban is more flexible and flow-based.
- **Planning:** Scrum plans work in batches at the start of each sprint; Kanban allows tasks to be added and prioritised continuously.
- **Delivery:** Scrum delivers at the end of each sprint; Kanban supports continuous delivery.
- **Flexibility:** Kanban is generally more adaptable to changing priorities.

In summary, Scrum is more structured and time-boxed, while Kanban is more flexible and focused on continuous workflow.

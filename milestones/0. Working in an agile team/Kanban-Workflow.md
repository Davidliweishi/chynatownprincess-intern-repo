🔍 Research & Learn

## How does a Kanban board work, and how does it help manage workflow?

A visual management tool that maps and lays out th eworkflow stages fo a project into columns. Each columns serves a different purpose, but represents the stages of a project process. 
For example - "To Do", "In Progress" and "Done", all are usinf cards to represent tasks associated with them. 


##What do the different columns on a Kanban board represent? (e.g., Backlog, In Progress, Blocked, Done)

1) Cards: represents individual work items, tasks or user stories.
2) Columns: Represents stages of a workflow, for example, backlog, designing, testing,etc.
3) WIP limits: limits the amount of cards in a column to stop overcapacity and reinforces focus.
4) Swimlanes: Horizontal rows to seperate different types of work, teams and priorities.
   
## How do tasks move through the board, and who is responsible for updating them?

Tasks are physically dragged from left to right, representing the progress towards completion. Team members are often responsible for 'pulling' new tasks into active work, only if they have capacity. Agan, this reinforces focus rather than overloading. 

## What are the benefits of limiting work in progress (WIP)?

Limiting the number of tasks allows developers to focus and create quality products. Rather than pushing more work onto the current workload, Kanban enforces a WIP limit ('work in progress'). If a column is fll, no new work is to be moved until another item is moved out - preventing bottlenecks.

## How does Kanban help manage priorities and avoid overload?

Kanban helps managing priorities and aovid overload by visualising the entire workflow, using WIP limits and 'pull' system to ensure work can only start if there is capacity to.
Teams can focus on current task at hand, and prevent themselves from multi-tasking and diminishing the quality of their work. 

How can you improve your workflow using Kanban principles?

visualizing tasks on a board, limiting work-in-progress (WIP) to prevent bottlenecks, and focusing on continuous flow.
